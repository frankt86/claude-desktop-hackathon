A seventy-five year arc, most of it in the last decade

The evolution of artificial intelligence.

The idea is old. The capability is new. Almost everything your students will use in practice arrived after 2017, and the curve is still bending upward.

Where the technology stands in 2026

Two things define this moment: pace, and the move to agents.

  • New frontier-class models now arrive every few days, each one cheaper and more capable than the version before it.
  • The systems have moved from answering a question to completing multi-step work across files and tools on their own. The industry calls these agents.
  • 2026 is widely described as the year agentic AI moved from demonstration to daily use in professional services.

You do not need to follow every release. You need to understand the direction, because your students will spend their careers inside it.

Sources: industry model-release trackers, 2026; Business Insider, "How the Big Four approached AI in 2025," Dec 2025.
How AI is changing accounting · The firms

The largest firms have moved first, and at scale.

  • Deloitte deployed Claude to its 470,000 employees worldwide in late 2025.
  • EY gave 80,000 tax professionals access to 150 AI agents, and plans to scale to 100,000 agents by 2028, investing more than $1 billion a year.
  • PwC has deployed roughly 25,000 AI agents across client work. KPMG runs a multi-agent audit platform with built-in accountability logs.
  • Deloitte projects its finance agents could cut costs in some functions by as much as 25%.
Sources: Business Insider / Yahoo Finance, Dec 2025; Financial World, Dec 2025.
How AI is changing accounting · The work

The center of gravity is moving from compliance to advisory.

  • Routine preparation, reconciliation, and first-pass document review are increasingly automated.
  • Audit teams use AI to review documentation and flag anomalies. Tax teams use it to cut research time on complex questions.
  • As routine work compresses, the profession's value shifts toward judgment, review, and client advice. The AICPA's own conclusion is that AI will not replace the profession, but it will change what an accountant does.

This is the part that should shape a curriculum. The tasks that once filled a first year are the tasks a machine now does in seconds.

Sources: AICPA & CPA.com, 2025 AI in Accounting Report, June 2025; Thomson Reuters, 2025.
How AI is changing accounting · The pipeline

The entry-level job your graduates expect is being redrawn.

  • Over two years the largest firms cut graduate intake as AI absorbed basic research and drafting: KPMG by 29%, Deloitte 18%, EY 11%, PwC 6%.
  • At the same time, more than 300,000 U.S. accountants and auditors have left the profession since 2020. Firms are closing that gap with technology, not headcount.
  • The role now emerging is the "digital senior": someone who can do the work and supervise the AI doing it.

Your graduates will not compete with AI. They will be hired to direct it and review it. That is a skill a program can teach.

Sources: Accountancy Age, "The Big Four's new favourite grad is AI," June 2025; U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics data, as reported 2022–2025.

What the research shows

Two findings worth carrying into class.

76.7% vs 47.4%

In the largest study of its kind, researchers tested AI against real accounting exams. Students beat the model. Within a year, the next model version had closed most of that gap. The lesson is the trajectory, not the snapshot.

Wood et al., Issues in Accounting Education, 2023. 327 authors, 186 institutions, 25,181 questions.
90% used it · 5% used it well

Across 2,500 employees and 1.4 million AI interactions over eight months, nearly everyone used AI, but only about one in twenty used it in sophisticated, high-impact ways. Usage is not skill, and skill is teachable.

KPMG & University of Texas at Austin, 2026 (via CPA Trendlines).

The differentiator is not access to AI. It is judgment about how to use it, and that is the part you teach.

What it means for the curriculum

The tie-out has not changed. Who performs the first pass has.

Teach less
Rote mechanics a machine now completes in seconds.
Teach more
Judgment. Review. Framing the right question. Detecting the wrong answer.
Redesign
Assessments that assume AI is available, not ones that hope it is not.

A take-home an AI can complete in four minutes was never testing what it was meant to test. This shift is what a growing body of accounting-education research now calls for.

Sources: "AI's Capabilities, Limitations, and Impact on Accounting Education," 2025 Issues in Accounting Education Best Paper; systematic reviews, Journal of Accounting Education, 2025–2026.
The shift for you

The role moves from gatekeeper to coach.

  • Think of AI as a capable but unreliable junior: fast, tireless, and in constant need of review.
  • The work moves from grading recall to coaching judgment.
  • The goal is neither to ban it nor to bless it. It is to teach students to supervise it, the way a manager reviews a first-year's file.

Students will use these tools regardless. Teaching them to use the tools well, in the open, is the version that actually serves them.

Two rules to teach from day one

For any accountant using AI.

1

Verify every number.

These systems can be confidently wrong, and they will invent citations that look real. Trace every figure back to its source before you trust it.

2

Treat output as a first draft.

Review it the way you would a first-year's work. Check the logic, check the tie-outs, then sign off. Never before.

If a student leaves your course with only these two habits, the course has done its job.

When and why · Chat

Chat: for answers and thinking.

A conversation with a capable colleague. Nothing to install, nothing saved — you ask, it answers, you judge.

  • Use it when you need an explanation, a first read, or a quick draft — the value is speed.
  • Use it when the task is one-off and not worth building anything around.
  • Use it when you want to think out loud: push back, ask "how confident are you?", iterate.

Its limit: you get advice, not work product — and the quality depends on how well you asked, every single time. The moment you type the same instructions twice, move up a rung.

The demo · One file, four rungs

Your junior staff just sent you this.

Account Number,Account Name,Amount (Dr +/Cr -) 1000-00,cash - operating ,152340.25 ACCT1010, Cash - Payroll,48200.00 1100,accounts receivable,287450.75 1200-00,INVENTORY,198760.00 2000-00,ACCOUNTS PAYABLE,-164230.40 ACCT2100, accrued liabilities,-58900.00 6100,rent expense,96000.00 ← …and this row appears twice

Three different account-number schemes, random capitalization, stray whitespace, debits and credits jammed into one signed column — plus a planted duplicate and an out-of-balance total hiding further down (sample-data/trial_balance_current.csv in your package). We handle it four ways, and each way teaches you a different thing Claude can be.

The demo · Rung 1 of 4 — Chat

Chat: the answer.

Regular Claude chat · Paste 10–15 rows of the messy file into the message
Here's an excerpt from a trial balance a junior staff member prepared. Identify everything wrong with it and explain each problem the way you'd explain it to the staff member so they learn.

No setup, no files, instant value — and its limit: you got an explanation, not a fixed file. Would you trust this without checking? Why not? The verification habit starts in minute one.

When and why · Chat + Skill

A skill: for anything you'll ask twice.

Your best prompt, saved and enforced — Claude follows it automatically, the same way every time, for you or anyone you share it with.

  • Use it when the same task keeps coming back: reviews, rubrics, workpaper standards.
  • Use it when consistency matters more than speed — forty students, one standard.
  • Use it when a rule must never be skipped — like "no silent fixes, ever."

Its limit: it still lives in chat — advice in, advice out. When the deliverable is a file, not a paragraph, that's Cowork.

The demo · Rung 2 of 4 — Chat + Skill

Skill: the repeatable answer.

Same chat · Then paste the same excerpt again and watch the skill apply itself
Help me create a skill called "TB cleanup standards". Whenever I give Claude a trial balance, it must: standardize account names and numbers, split signed amounts into debit/credit columns, verify the TB balances, and always produce an Exceptions list documenting every change and why. No silent fixes, ever.

The prompt you perfected is now an asset. A skill is how you hand your prompting expertise to 40 students at once — or to yourself next semester.

When and why · Cowork

Cowork: when you need the work done.

Claude as an agent: it opens your files, works multi-step, reaches live data, and hands back a finished file.

  • Use it when the deliverable is an Excel workbook, a Word memo, a PDF — a real artifact, not a paragraph.
  • Use it when the job takes multiple steps: clean, verify, compare, format, document.
  • Use it when you need live outside data — like SEC filings through the EDGAR connector.

Its limit: next month, you'd have to ask all over again. When the same deliverable will repeat, stop asking for the answer — ask for the machine. That's Code.

The demo · Rung 3 of 4 — Cowork

Cowork: the deliverable.

Switch to Cowork · Attach the full trial_balance_current.csv
Clean up this trial balance following proper workpaper standards: trim whitespace, standardize account names to title case, fix inconsistent account numbers, split the signed Amount column into Debit and Credit. Verify it balances — if not, identify the suspicious entries. Output an Excel workbook with three tabs: Clean TB, Summary by Category, and Exceptions listing every change you made and why.

Claude is now doing, not advising — it catches the planted duplicate and the out-of-balance entry, and the output is a real .xlsx you open live. This is the jump from "Chat & Skills" to "Cowork."

When and why · Cowork + Code

Code: when the task repeats forever.

Claude writes a program you keep. You don't write the code — you commission it, then review it like everything else Claude hands you.

  • Use it when the process recurs on a schedule: month-end, quarter-end, every semester.
  • Use it when it's the same analysis with different inputs — any ticker, any trial balance.
  • Use it when reruns must be instant and identical — seconds, zero drift, and a script you can inspect.

If it runs once, Cowork was enough. If it runs every month, build the machine — minutes to build, seconds to rerun, and the tool is yours to keep.

The demo · Rung 4 of 4 — Cowork + Code

Code: the machine.

Cowork or Claude Code · Both sample-data files attached
Write a Python script called monthly_variance.py that cleans any trial balance CSV like the one attached, verifies it balances, compares it to a prior period file, and writes the same three-tab Excel workbook. Run it on the two files in sample-data and show me the exceptions it found.

Then rerun it and time it. A few minutes to build, two seconds to rerun. Next month-end: drop in the new file, run one command. Cowork gave you the answer; Code gave you the answer plus the machine.

Challenge A · The 10-K, four ways · Live SEC data

Pick one public company your team knows.

1

Chat — the 5-minute briefing ~10 min

Have Claude explain the company's business model and its three biggest financial-statement risks for an intermediate-accounting student. Then push back: "how confident are you in those figures?" — memory versus verification is the point.

2

Skill — the "10-K digest" ~15 min

Build a skill that turns any company name into a one-page partner memo: top risk factors, MD&A highlights, red flags, a "verify these figures" list, a DRAFT label. Test it on two companies — notice the consistency.

3

Cowork — the live ratio workbook ~20 min

Using the SEC EDGAR connector, pull real filing figures for your company and a competitor into an Excel workbook — ratios side by side, a source filing URL for every number. Click one URL and tie a number back to the 10-K.

4

Code — the any-ticker machine stretch

A Python script that takes any ticker and produces the same ratio workbook from the SEC EDGAR API. Prove it on a company you haven't analyzed yet.

Full copy-paste prompts for every level: Challenges.docx → Challenge A.

Challenge B · Month-end rescue · Local files, no connector

The partner wants a flux analysis by end of day.

1

Chat — the teaching moment ~10 min

Paste 10–15 rows of trial_balance_current.csv and have Claude find everything wrong — formatting, structure, suspicious entries — explained so the junior staffer learns. Compare against what your team spotted first.

2

Skill — workpaper standards ~15 min

Encode your review standards as the "TB cleanup standards" skill: standardize names and numbers, split debits/credits, verify the balance, and always produce an Exceptions list. No silent fixes, ever — the audit trail, made non-negotiable.

3

Cowork — the flux workbook ~20 min

Attach both trial balances. Claude cleans the messy one, then builds the variance analysis: dollar and percent change per account, items over 10% and $5,000 flagged with a plausible explanation to confirm or reject, plus an Exceptions tab. Did it catch the planted duplicate?

4

Code — next month in two seconds stretch

monthly_variance.py — everything Level 3 did, for any pair of trial balance CSVs. Run it, check it matches your workbook, rerun it, time it.

Full copy-paste prompts for every level: Challenges.docx → Challenge B.

Show and tell · Two minutes per team

Show us what you built.

  • The deliverable. Put it on the screen — the analysis, the workpaper, the script. Whatever rung you reached.
  • One number you verified. Show the figure and the source you traced it to. This is the habit we came to build.
  • One surprise. Where was the AI better than you expected — and where was it confidently wrong?

The rung you reached matters less than what you noticed on the way up. The wrong answers you caught are worth as much as the right ones you got.

What to take back

Two things for your syllabus.

  • The verification habit. Teach it as a reflex, the way you already teach the tie-out.
  • The ladder. Match the rung to the moment. A discussion prompt is rung one. A grading rubric is rung two. A case deliverable is rung three. A research pipeline is rung four.

You do not need to become an engineer. You need to become the reviewer your students will be asked to be, and to teach them to be it too.
